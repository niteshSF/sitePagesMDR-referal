Heterodox Systems

Traditional Indian Philosophy

Indian philosophy is traditionally divided into two broad streams: Āstika (orthodox) and Nāstika (heterodox) systems. The distinction rests not on theism but on the acceptance of the Vedas as authoritative. The six classical darśanas — Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Pūrva Mīmāṃsā, and Uttara Mīmāṃsā (Vedānta) — are considered orthodox, as they base their thought on the Vedas, even while differing in interpretation. The heterodox systems — Buddhism, Jainism, and Cārvāka — reject Vedic authority, though they offer rich philosophical frameworks of their own. Together, these schools represent the diversity of inquiry in Bhāratīya thought, ranging from rigorous logic and metaphysics to ethics, liberation, and spiritual practice.
