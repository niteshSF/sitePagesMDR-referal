पूर्व मीमांसा - Pūrva Mīmāṃsā

Pūrva Mīmāṃsā, traditionally attributed to Jaimini, is one of the six orthodox systems of Indian philosophy. The term Mīmāṃsā means “inquiry” or “reflection,” and the Pūrva (earlier) section focuses on the ritual portions of the Veda (karma-kāṇḍa). Its central concern is the correct performance of yajñas (sacrifices) and the eternal authority of the Vedic injunctions. Unlike systems that emphasize liberation through knowledge or meditation, Pūrva Mīmāṃsā holds that dharma is realized primarily through ritual action. It asserts that the Veda is eternal, authorless (apauruṣeya), and infallible, serving as the supreme guide for right conduct. The system also introduces elaborate rules of hermeneutics (interpretation) to resolve contradictions and interpret Vedic passages consistently.
Philosophically, it maintains that the soul (ātman) is eternal, action (karma) leaves unseen results (apūrva), and heaven or desired fruits are obtained through the precise execution of Vedic rituals. Liberation (mokṣa), according to some later Mīmāṃsakas, is freedom from pain, achieved when the soul is released from the cycle of rebirth, though the school mainly emphasizes ritual efficacy over metaphysics.
