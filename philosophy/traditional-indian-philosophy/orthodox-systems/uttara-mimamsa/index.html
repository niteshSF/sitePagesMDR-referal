Uttara Mīmāṃsā

Vedānta, also known as Uttara Mīmāṃsā, is the culminating system of Indian philosophy, drawing its authority from the Upaniṣads, the Brahma Sūtras of Bādarāyaṇa, and the Bhagavad Gītā. Unlike Pūrva Mīmāṃsā, which emphasizes ritual (karma), Vedānta highlights knowledge (jñāna) as the path to liberation (mokṣa), teaching that Brahman is the ultimate reality and the self (ātman) is either identical with or related to it. Over time, Vedānta developed into diverse schools: Advaita (non-dualism, Śaṅkara), Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non-dualism, Rāmānuja), Dvaita (dualism, Madhva), Dvaitādvaita (dualistic non-dualism, Nimbārka), Śuddhādvaita (pure non-dualism, Vallabha), and Acintya-bhedābheda (inconceivable oneness and difference, Caitanya). Together, these schools shaped the metaphysical, devotional, and spiritual foundations of later Hindu thought.
