Algebra, a vital branch of mathematics, has its origins deeply rooted in the ancient knowledge systems of Bharat, where it was known as "Bijagaņita," meaning "computation with seeds." This term reflects the essence of algebra, emphasizing problem-solving through symbols and equations. Ancient Bharatiya mathematicians, such as Aryabhața, Brahmagupta, and Bhāskarācārya, made significant advancements in algebraic thought, contributing foundational concepts like the sine function, techniques for solving indeterminate equations and methods for quadratic equations. Their works, often presented in poetic verse, not only facilitated memorization but also integrated mathematics into the cultural and spiritual fabric of society. Algebra's applications extended to fields such as astronomy, architecture and commerce, highlighting its practical relevance. The legacy of Bharatiya algebra profoundly influenced the development of mathematical thought in the Islamic world and Europe, celebrating the innovative spirit of ancient Bharat and its rich intellectual heritage.
