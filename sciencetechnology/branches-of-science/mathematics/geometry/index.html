Geometry, known as Jyamitih or Śulba-Vidyā or Kşetraganita in ancient Bharat, is deeply rooted in the spiritual and ritualistic practices of Vedic culture. This mathematical discipline was essential for constructing altars, temples, and urban layouts, demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of shapes, sizes, and spatial relationships. The foundational texts, the Sulba Sūtras, dating back to around 800-200 BCE, systematically explored geometric principles, including the early formulation of the Pythagorean theorem. The meticulous calculations required for constructing altars, designed to reflect the cosmos, highlight the connection between geometry and Vedic rituals. Beyond religious contexts, geometric knowledge influenced architecture and urban planning, as seen in the cities of Mohenjo-Daro and Harappa, as well as the design of iconic temples like those in Khajuraho. The legacy of Bharatiya geometry extends to global mathematics, contributing foundational concepts such as zero, the decimal system, and the spread of geometric knowledge through trade and scholarship, ultimately shaping the renaissance and scientific revolution. Figures like Aryabhața and Brahmagupta exemplify the remarkable advancements in geometry, showcasing its profound influence on both intellectual and cultural achievements in ancient Bharat.
