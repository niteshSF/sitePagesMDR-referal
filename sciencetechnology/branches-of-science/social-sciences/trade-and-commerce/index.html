Ancient Bharat thrived as a global hub for trade and commerce, with its goods in high demand across Asia, Europe, and Africa, reflecting a civilization deeply intertwined with spiritual, cultural, and economic dynamics. Transitioning from a barter system to a currency-based economy, the introduction of metal coins streamlined transactions, as highlighted in texts like the Manusmrti and the Arthaśāstra. The region's strategic location facilitated extensive maritime trade routes connecting Bharat to Egypt, Rome, China, and Southeast Asia, promoting not only the exchange of spices and textiles but also the spread of ideas and culture, notably Buddhism. Vedic texts underscore the importance of trade, mentioning early maritime commerce and the protection of traders, while standardized weights and measures ensured fair practices. Marketplaces, known as āpaņas, served as bustling centres regulated by kings, emphasizing the importance of trade as a cornerstone of society. The enduring legacy of ancient Bharat's trade practices continues to influence modern commerce, showcasing its sophisticated understanding of economic principles and ethical trading.
